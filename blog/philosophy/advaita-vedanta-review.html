Advaita Vedanta

By Rithwik Nukala
January 2, 2026

These are my personal notes and reflections on the philosophy of Advaita Vedanta, inspired by the teachings of Swami Sarvapriyananda as well as talking to my friends.

Video Reference: Watch on YouTube

1. The Nature of Reality: Seeing All Beings in Myself

If we posit that everything we feel through our five senses is merely our consciousness's perception, then someone who has fully internalized this—and put it into practice—is theoretically able to see beyond our physical world to what is considered "God."

I find this idea fascinating because it not only reconciles how we, as humans, understand our environment, but it also provides a direct path to reach the divine.

The Dream Analogy

Are our dreams just an extension of what our consciousness experiences in the waking world? If we conclude that everything we see in daily life isn't "real" (in the material sense) but is actually a manifestation of consciousness, then dreams might just be another layer of this manifestation.

This leads to a diverging idea, somewhat similar to the movie Inception: our dreams could have dreams of their own. However, this raises a question of consistency. In order for consciousness to construct the scenarios we live in, the mind must have background knowledge of the world. If every person experiences the same reality I do, their consciousness must share similar background experiences to fabricate the world in the same way.

The Solipsism Trap

Initially, I assumed that perhaps only the objects in my life are manifestations of my consciousness. But what if the animals and people are also just manifestations?

If I take this thought to the extreme, it might explain why Vedanta philosophy says that "everyone is just me." If they are manifestations of my consciousness, then in reality, they are me.

Note: This sounds similar to the "Simulation Theory." However, after deeper study, I realized the difference: Simulation theory implies a computer or external construct, whereas Vedanta implies a unified field of Consciousness.

2. The Search for the Real

If the mind is the source of our consciousness, does that mean our mind is the one true real thing? Wait—we as humans defined the "mind" as a physical/conceptual object to store thoughts. If we go one step deeper, the only real thing in our world is Consciousness itself.

The Paradox of Enlightenment

Question: If one day you attain enlightenment and see God, that experience happens within consciousness. But if we say that everything we experience isn't real and only consciousness is real, where do we draw the line? Where does enlightenment end and the "figment" of enlightenment begin?

A helpful example from the video addresses this:

Suppose someone says, "Beyond what my eye can see is something crazy that isn’t Earth." If you reach that point, you are no longer on Earth. Similarly, can we say "Beyond my consciousness exists matter"?

The answer is no. Since everything we experience must be experienced in consciousness, we cannot pick and choose. We cannot step "outside" consciousness to verify a world outside it.

As Swami Sarvapriyananda says:

"That which appears must be false; In which it appears, that must be true."

Does this mean physical objects (like the Earth) are false, or that everything—people, animals, and objects—are false appearances, while the Consciousness in which they appear is the only Truth?

3. The Shift: Seeing Myself in All Beings

In the first part, we explored the idea that "all beings are myself" (solipsism/projections). Now we face the inverse: I exist in everything else.

This seems to contradict the previous statement. If I exist in everything, it implies those "things" have a reality to hold me. However, the true goal of Advaita Vedanta isn't to teach that the world is fake while "I" (the ego) am the only real thing. The goal is to show that all consciousnesses are One.

Everyone else exists as different forms of ourselves, living different lives.

The Ego Problem

I initially liked the idea that "all beings are superimpositions of myself" because it made me feel important—like I was the main character. But this is the exact opposite of what Vedanta teaches. One of the byproducts of seeing everyone as One is the removal of the ego. We stop caring about "ourselves" (the small self) first.

It is important to remember that while everyone looks like an outsider to me—even my family—they are living their own lives with their own vivid experiences.

4. Reconciling Devotion and Non-Dualism

Question: A famous non-dualist is Ramana Maharshi. I know him from his Bhakti (devotional) songs directed toward Shiva (Arunachala). Doesn’t the idea of a God existing directly contradict the idea of non-dualism (that we ourselves are God)?

Answer: Non-dualism doesn’t deny duality; it is the ground on which duality is staged.

I realized I was still divided by the roles/labels we place on one another. When considering everyone to be One Self, the distinction blurs. We are our own teacher, our own God, and our own Self.

  • Ramana Maharshi prays to Arunachala because he sees the mountain as himself, yet also as his teacher.
  • By praying to the mountain, he is validating the underlying assumption that everything is One: he is praying to his teacher, God, and himself simultaneously.

5. Understanding Moksha (Liberation)

We can think of Moksha not as "attaining" something new, but as uncovering our true self. The word is important: we are rediscovering, not becoming.

The fundamental idea of Jeevan-Mukthi (liberation while living) is to become "egotistical on a cosmic scale"—realizing that the separate "you" is illusory and the only real thing is the Atman (Self/Consciousness).

The Deep Sleep Analogy

When we sleep (dreamless sleep), we are disconnected from the world. Our senses shut off. To the waking world, we are effectively gone. Yet, in that state, we are in bliss and peace. We don't care about the world because, for us, it doesn't exist. This state is closest to the true nature of the Atman.

6. Levels of Reality and "The Movie"

How do we reconcile daily life with the belief that the world is illusory? Vedanta describes levels of reality:

  1. Paramarthika (Absolute Reality): The one true reality. Only the Atman exists.
  2. Vyavaharika (Empirical Reality): The shared base reality. The world we live in and operate within.
  3. Pratibhasika (Subjective Reality): Illusions, hallucinations, or dreams superimposed onto the base reality.

The Divine Game (Leela)

Think of it like a movie. When we watch a movie, we know the characters aren't real. We don't call the police on the movie villain. We know it's "fake," but we immerse ourselves in it anyway.

Achieving Jeevan-Mukthi doesn't mean disregarding the world. It means treating it like a movie. We play along. This is Leela—the divine play.

Final Thoughts

The point of Yoga or religious practice is to clear the mind of distractions so you can see this perspective. It is a constant reminder not to fall into the trap of believing the projection is the only reality.

Whether it is Christianity teaching that control is an illusion (surrender to God) or Vedanta teaching that the separate self is an illusion, the path moves toward the same goal: clearing the mind to remember who we truly are.
